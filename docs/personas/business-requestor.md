# Persona: Business Requestor

## Role Overview

**Common Titles:** Product Manager, Product Owner, Business Analyst, Business Sponsor, Director of Operations, VP of [Business Unit], Project Sponsor, Functional Lead
**Reports To:** Business unit leadership (COO, CMO, CFO, or General Manager)
**Sphere of Influence:** Requirements definition, budget advocacy, stakeholder alignment within the business unit

---

## Background Profile

Background in the business domain — finance, marketing, operations, customer experience, supply chain — rather than technology. May have 5–20 years in their business discipline with increasing digital literacy but not technical depth. Increasingly expected to own digital products and technology initiatives as "the business" in business-IT partnerships. May hold PMP, CBAP, SAFe certifications, or an MBA. Defines success in terms of business outcomes: revenue, cost savings, customer satisfaction, and operational efficiency — not technical metrics.

---

## Core Knowledge Domains

- Their business domain deeply (finance, HR, supply chain, marketing, etc.)
- Business process modeling and workflow design
- Requirements gathering and user story creation
- Vendor/product evaluation and business case development
- Project management and stakeholder communication
- Regulatory requirements specific to their domain (HIPAA, SOX, PCI-DSS as applicable)
- Customer or end-user needs and behaviors
- Budget planning and cost justification
- Change management and user adoption

---

## Primary Goals & Objectives

1. **Solve a business problem or capture an opportunity** — the technology is the means, not the end
2. **Deliver on time and within budget** — project sponsors are accountable for outcomes
3. **Drive adoption** — a solution that users don't use is a failure, regardless of technical quality
4. **Demonstrate ROI** — justify the investment to their leadership
5. **Minimize disruption** — new systems should improve workflows, not create new burdens
6. **Maintain compliance** — ensure solutions don't violate regulatory or policy requirements
7. **Build a stronger relationship with IT** — position themselves as a trusted technology partner

---

## Pain Points & Frustrations

- IT projects that deliver technically correct solutions that miss the business need
- Requirements that get "lost in translation" between business language and technical specification
- Projects that take far longer and cost more than estimated
- Being told "no" or "that's not how it works" without alternatives being offered
- Technology jargon that excludes them from meaningful participation in decisions
- Scope creep that they didn't intend to introduce
- Solutions that solve the wrong problem because IT didn't ask enough questions
- Shadow IT decisions forced on them when IT can't move fast enough
- Lack of business continuity planning — "what happens when this breaks?"

---

## Decision-Making Style

**Outcome-oriented:** Evaluates options based on whether they achieve the desired business result, not on technical elegance
**Risk-averse about operations:** Doesn't want existing processes disrupted without a clear plan
**ROI-conscious:** Every decision needs a business case; will lose executive support without one
**Consensus-builder internally:** Must align their own stakeholders (end users, finance, compliance) before committing
**Timeline-sensitive:** Has external commitments (regulatory, product launch, fiscal calendar) that drive urgency

---

## Communication Preferences

- **Lead with the "so what" for the business** — frame everything in terms of what it means for their team, their customers, or their operations
- **Avoid acronyms and technical terminology** — or define them explicitly when unavoidable
- **Use AIDA for persuasion** — Attention → Interest → Desire → Action works well when seeking buy-in
- **Connect to their business KPIs** — if it doesn't affect revenue, cost, customer satisfaction, or compliance, they'll deprioritize it
- **Show options, not mandates** — they want to feel agency in decisions even when technical options are constrained
- **Written summaries** — business requestors often need to re-present information to their own stakeholders; give them something they can share

---

## Best Framing Approaches

| Situation | Recommended Format |
|-----------|-------------------|
| Proposing a new solution | **AIDA** (capture attention with the problem, build desire for the solution) |
| Escalating a risk or issue | **SCQA** (Situation → Complication → Question → Answer) |
| Presenting project status | **What, So What, Now What** |
| Building a business case | **PAR** (Problem → Action → Result with quantified outcomes) |
| Prioritizing a roadmap | **Now, Next, Later** with business impact for each bucket |

**AIDA example (proposing an automation solution):**
> *Attention:* Your team is spending 800 person-hours per month manually reconciling invoices — that's 5 FTE-equivalent of time.
> *Interest:* Three of your peers in comparable organizations have automated this with off-the-shelf tools, reducing reconciliation time by 85%.
> *Desire:* An automated workflow would free your analysts to focus on exceptions, vendor relationships, and strategic work — not data entry.
> *Action:* We're proposing a 6-week pilot starting next quarter. Can we schedule 30 minutes to walk through the approach?

**PAR example (business case summary):**
> *Problem:* Customer onboarding took 14 days on average, causing a 23% drop-off rate before first transaction.
> *Action:* We implemented a digital onboarding portal with automated identity verification and e-signature.
> *Result:* Onboarding time reduced to 2 days, drop-off rate fell to 8%, and we retained an estimated $4.2M in revenue in the first year.

## Key Vocabulary (Use These Terms)

- Business case, ROI, payback period
- User adoption, change management, training
- Process improvement, efficiency gain, time savings
- Stakeholders, end users, sponsors
- Requirements, acceptance criteria, user stories, use cases
- Scope, deliverables, milestones, go-live date
- Compliance, audit trail, regulatory requirement
- KPIs, SLAs, metrics, dashboards
- Risk mitigation, fallback plan, business continuity
- Phase 1, MVP, pilot program, proof of concept

---

## Motivators

- **Solving the problem they brought to IT** — they came with a real need; they want it solved
- **Recognition for delivering business outcomes** — credit for a successful project
- **Speed** — faster delivery is almost always better from a business perspective
- **Simplicity** — solutions that their users can understand and use without extensive training
- **Partnership** — feeling like IT is working with them, not just telling them what's possible
- **Transparency** — knowing what's happening, why it's taking time, and what risks exist

---

## Common Objections

- *"Why is this taking so long?"* — Explain complexity in business terms; show the delivery plan
- *"Can't we just do it the simple way?"* — Understand what "simple" means to them; sometimes it's valid
- *"We've been doing it this way for years; why change now?"* — Connect change to a compelling business event (regulation, competitor, cost)
- *"That sounds expensive."* — Show the cost of inaction (current inefficiency, risk exposure, competitive disadvantage)
- *"IT always says no."* — Acknowledge this perception directly; focus on what is possible and how to get there

---

## What to Avoid

- Saying "that's not technically possible" without exploring alternatives
- Presenting technical solutions without connecting them to business outcomes
- Asking business requestors to make technical decisions they're not equipped to make
- Over-engineering requirements gathering with excessive formality that slows discovery
- Delivering exactly what was asked for without validating that it solves the actual problem
- Leaving business requestors out of go-live and launch planning — adoption is their responsibility
