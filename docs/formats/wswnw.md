# What, So What, Now What Framework

## Overview
The "What, So What, Now What" framework is a structured reflection and analysis method that helps individuals and teams process information, understand implications, and determine actionable next steps. Originally developed for educational reflection, it's widely used in business analysis, incident response, project retrospectives, and strategic planning.

## Framework Structure

### What (Observation/Description)
**Purpose**: Objective observation and data collection  
**Focus**: Facts, events, and concrete information  
**Questions to Ask**:
- What happened?
- What did we observe?
- What are the facts?
- What data do we have?

### So What (Analysis/Interpretation)
**Purpose**: Understanding significance and implications  
**Focus**: Meaning, patterns, and impact analysis  
**Questions to Ask**:
- Why is this important?
- What are the implications?
- What patterns do we see?
- How does this affect our goals?

### Now What (Action/Application)
**Purpose**: Determining next steps and actions  
**Focus**: Decisions, plans, and implementation  
**Questions to Ask**:
- What should we do next?
- How will we apply this learning?
- What changes need to be made?
- Who needs to be involved?

## Sample Prompt Template

```
Please analyze the following situation using the "What, So What, Now What" framework:

**Context**: [Describe the situation, project, or event being analyzed]

**Background Information**:
- [Relevant details, timeline, stakeholders involved]
- [Any constraints, goals, or success criteria]

**Analysis Request**: [Specific focus area or question to address]

**Output Format**:

## What (Observations)
**Objective Facts**:
- [List concrete observations, data points, or events]
- [Include quantitative metrics where available]
- [Note timeline and sequence of events]

**Key Data Points**:
- [Relevant statistics, measurements, or feedback]
- [Stakeholder inputs or comments]

## So What (Analysis)
**Significance**:
- [Why these observations matter]
- [Impact on objectives or stakeholders]

**Patterns & Trends**:
- [What patterns emerge from the data]
- [Connections to broader themes or issues]

**Implications**:
- [Short-term and long-term consequences]
- [Risks and opportunities identified]

## Now What (Actions)
**Immediate Actions** (1-2 weeks):
- [Specific steps to take right away]
- [Who is responsible for each action]

**Short-term Plans** (1-3 months):
- [Strategic adjustments or improvements]
- [Process changes or new initiatives]

**Long-term Considerations** (3+ months):
- [Systemic changes or strategic shifts]
- [Monitoring and evaluation plans]

**Success Metrics**:
- [How we'll measure progress and success]
```

## Example Application: Software Project Retrospective

### Context
A development team just completed a major software release that was delivered 2 weeks late and had several critical bugs discovered in production.

### Background Information
- 6-month project with 8 developers
- Original deadline: March 1st, Actual delivery: March 15th
- 3 critical bugs found in first week of production
- Customer satisfaction score: 6.5/10 (target was 8.5/10)
- Budget overrun: 15%

## What (Observations)

**Objective Facts**:
- Project delivered 2 weeks (14 days) past deadline
- 3 critical production bugs discovered within 7 days of release
- Budget exceeded planned amount by 15% ($45,000 over $300,000 budget)
- Customer satisfaction survey: 6.5/10 average score
- 47 total bugs logged in first month (normal baseline: 20-25)
- Code review completion rate: 73% (target: 90%)

**Key Data Points**:
- Final testing phase extended from 2 weeks to 4 weeks
- 23% of planned unit tests were skipped due to time pressure
- 2 team members worked overtime (>50 hours/week) for final 6 weeks
- 5 scope changes requested during development phase
- Integration testing revealed 12 blocking issues

## So What (Analysis)

**Significance**:
- Late delivery impacts customer trust and market positioning
- Quality issues directly affect user experience and support costs
- Budget overrun reduces project ROI and affects future funding
- Team burnout risks affecting future project performance

**Patterns & Trends**:
- Testing shortcuts consistently lead to production issues
- Scope changes during development create cascading delays
- Insufficient code review coverage correlates with bug density
- Overtime usage indicates poor resource planning or unrealistic timelines

**Implications**:
- **Short-term**: Increased support burden, potential customer churn, team morale issues
- **Long-term**: Reputation damage, reduced confidence in delivery estimates, technical debt accumulation
- **Process**: Current planning and quality gates are insufficient for project complexity

## Now What (Actions)

**Immediate Actions** (1-2 weeks):
- **Hot-fix critical bugs**: Development team to prioritize production issues (Owner: Lead Developer)
- **Customer communication**: Send status update and improvement plan to affected customers (Owner: Product Manager)
- **Team retrospective**: Conduct detailed retrospective session with all team members (Owner: Scrum Master)

**Short-term Plans** (1-3 months):
- **Testing process overhaul**: Implement mandatory testing gates with no skip provisions (Owner: QA Lead)
- **Code review standards**: Establish and enforce 90% code review completion requirement (Owner: Tech Lead)
- **Scope change controls**: Implement formal change request process with impact assessment (Owner: Project Manager)
- **Capacity planning**: Review and adjust estimation processes based on historical data (Owner: Engineering Manager)

**Long-term Considerations** (3+ months):
- **Development methodology review**: Evaluate current Agile practices and implement improvements
- **Team skills assessment**: Identify training needs and implement development programs
- **Tooling improvements**: Invest in automated testing and CI/CD pipeline enhancements
- **Client expectation management**: Establish clearer communication protocols for future projects

**Success Metrics**:
- Reduce production bugs by 60% in next release
- Achieve 95% code review completion rate
- Deliver next project within 5% of timeline estimate
- Improve customer satisfaction to 8.0+ average score

## Best Practices

### For Effective Analysis
1. **Separate Facts from Opinions**: Keep "What" section purely observational
2. **Dig Deep in "So What"**: Don't settle for surface-level analysis
3. **Be Specific in "Now What"**: Avoid vague action items
4. **Include Timelines**: Assign deadlines and owners to actions
5. **Follow Up**: Schedule review sessions to assess progress

### Common Mistakes to Avoid
- Jumping to solutions without proper analysis
- Mixing observations with interpretations in "What" section
- Creating action items that aren't specific or measurable
- Focusing only on problems rather than opportunities
- Not assigning clear ownership for next steps

## Integration with Other Frameworks

### Root Cause Analysis
- **What**: Problem description and symptoms
- **So What**: Impact analysis and causal relationships
- **Now What**: Corrective and preventive actions

### Design Thinking
- **What**: User research and observation data
- **So What**: Insight synthesis and opportunity identification
- **Now What**: Ideation and prototyping plans

### Agile Retrospectives
- **What**: Sprint performance data and team observations
- **So What**: Analysis of what worked well and what didn't
- **Now What**: Action items for next sprint improvement

## Facilitation Tips

### Leading a "What, So What, Now What" Session
1. **Set clear ground rules**: Emphasize objectivity in the "What" phase
2. **Use visual aids**: Employ whiteboards or digital tools to capture inputs
3. **Encourage participation**: Ensure all voices are heard in each phase
4. **Time-box each section**: Allocate appropriate time for each phase
5. **Document everything**: Capture all insights and assign action items
6. **Schedule follow-up**: Plan check-ins to review progress on actions
